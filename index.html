What actually works

Six solutions, already running at scale

01 · Source

Segregation at the bin

Split waste into wet (kitchen, garden), dry (plastic, paper, metal), and hazardous (batteries, medicine, e-waste) right where it's generated. Every downstream solution below depends on this one habit.

02 · Home

Home & community composting

A simple pot, pit, or bokashi bin turns kitchen waste into usable soil in 30–45 days. Apartment complexes running shared composters have cut their outgoing wet waste to near zero.

03 · Energy

Biogas from kitchen waste

Small biogas plants (even household-sized units) digest food waste anaerobically, producing cooking gas and slurry fertilizer. Common now in schools, hostels, and market complexes.

04 · Materials

Dry waste recovery & recycling

Dry waste collection centers sort plastic, paper, glass, and metal for sale to recyclers — turning a cost center into a small revenue stream for local waste-picker cooperatives.

05 · Hazard

Dedicated e-waste & battery drop-offs

Phones, batteries, and CFL bulbs need separate authorized collection points, since they leach heavy metals if landfilled or burned with regular trash.

06 · Behaviour

Pay-as-you-throw + fines

Charging households by the bag for mixed (unsegregated) waste, while collecting segregated waste free, is the single policy lever that has moved segregation rates fastest.

Proof it scales

Indore: from India's dirtiest to its cleanest city

Indore rebuilt its entire waste system around one rule: 100% source segregation into six categories, collected by GPS-tracked door-to-door vehicles, processed at a dedicated bio-CNG plant fed by the city's own wet waste.

The underlying logic

Every solution above is just 3R, applied

R1

Reduce

Refuse single-use plastic, buy in bulk, choose repairable products. The cheapest waste to manage is the waste never created.

R2

Reuse

Refill containers, donate usable items, repair before replacing. Extends an item's working life before it ever becomes waste.

R3

Recycle & Recover

For what's left: segregate, compost the organic share, recycle the dry share, and safely dispose of the hazardous share.
